They run through the same back-of-body corridor — and there's a genuine anatomical finding behind why they look alike on the chart. But that's where I stop the equals sign, and the distinction is worth keeping precise.

**The convergence (real, tier-tagged):**

Langevin & Yandow (2002, PMID 12467083) found that roughly 80% of acupuncture points coincide with connective-tissue planes — **but that was demonstrated in the human arm they dissected.** For the posterior chain, the overlap is plausible by anatomical extension, not yet directly shown across the back. So the *location* plausibly overlaps — same corridor, similar surface territory — and that's a genuinely interesting anatomical finding. It's not hand-waving.

**The divergence (where the equals sign stops):**

The SBL is a **mechanical-force map.** It describes how tensile load transmits along continuous fascial tissue — from plantar fascia through calf, hamstring, erector spinae, up to the scalp. That's a testable, tested claim: Wilke et al. (2016, PMID 26281953) verified all three of its transitions across 14 studies. Strong evidence. I can stand on it.

The Bladder meridian is a **qi map from a different tradition** with a different mechanism — flow of qi along a channel, not mechanical force along fascia. Those are not the same claim dressed in different clothes. They're different kinds of explanation entirely.

Myers himself is explicit on this: he calls his myofascial meridians and acupuncture meridians **lines of latitude and longitude** — two different coordinate systems laid over the same globe. Two lines of latitude can cross a mountain range at the same point without being the same line.

**The one-liner that does the most work here:**

> Same neighborhood, different map. Overlap in *where*, not in *why.*

**Why the distinction earns you something:**

If you flatten the two maps into one, you lose the ability to use either honestly. You can't stand behind the Bladder meridian's qi theory with Wilke's fascial evidence — that's laundering a traditional claim through a mechanical study. And you can't claim the SBL is "validated" by TCM's longevity — that's running it the other direction. Both maps become muddier.

Keep them separate and you get to use both: the SBL for the biomechanical pull along the posterior chain (Tier 1, confirmed), and the Bladder meridian for the traditional framework your client or your training might work within (Tier 2 location, traditional mechanism). One finding, two maps, each doing what it actually does.
