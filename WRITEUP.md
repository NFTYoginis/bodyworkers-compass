# Writeup — The Design Rationale

## What this is

Bodyworker's Compass reads one finding across four ways of mapping the
body — joint anatomy, Anatomy Trains fascia, the meridians, and the Thai Sen lines
— and keeps the finding intact as it crosses all four. You can describe the finding
in words or simply show it the image: a client's standing posture, a meridian
chart, an Anatomy Trains diagram. It names what each map sees, side by side, in one
pass. The design problem behind it was never "how do we make Claude recite four
anatomies." Claude can already do that, softly, on its own. The problem was how to
build the *one practitioner in a thousand* who is genuinely fluent across all four
maps and disciplined enough not to blur them — and that is a question of authority
and architecture, not recall.

## Why four maps, and why a guide that reads all of them is rare

Most body tools speak one language. The Western biomechanics tool talks load and
levers. The fascial tool talks lines of pull. The TCM tool talks channels. The Thai
tradition talks Sen. A practitioner working a chronic low back is, in practice,
already moving between all of them — and getting no help holding them together. The
rare move is not collecting four vocabularies; it is carrying a *single* finding
across all four without smuggling one map's claims into another. That move takes
someone who has actually worked inside the lineages. Gabe Yoga trained under
Pattabhi Jois and B.K.S. Iyengar in 2005, studied Pichest Boonthume's Thai tradition
in Chiang Mai, and spent three decades synthesizing the fascial and meridian maps
into the Joint Dialogue Method. The guide is the externalization of that fluency —
which is why it can read four maps at once and a single-map tool can't.

## The four-map architecture

The guide treats each map as a coordinate system over the same terrain — Thomas
Myers' own image of "latitude and longitude," which governs the whole tool. Joint
anatomy is the Tier-1 ground truth everything else is checked against. Anatomy
Trains is read line by line, because the evidence is not uniform even within it. The
meridian map is read in two strictly separated layers — a real *location*
correlation to connective-tissue planes, and an energetic theory that the location
finding does not validate. The Thai Sen map is named with its physical territory and
deferred, in its depth, to the living lineage. Four maps, four registers, one
terrain — connected on the territory they share, each kept honestly in its own
register.

## The cross-system vocabulary bridge — the actual skill

The distinctive engineering is the **bridge**: a register-neutral vocabulary, drawn
from the Joint Dialogue Method, that lets a finding travel. *Short* and *Long*
replace "tight" and "weak" — words for what tissue is doing that commit to no
mechanism, so every map can receive them. *The Four Words* (Background · Annoyed ·
Pain · Injury) are how the body reports underneath all four maps. *Load vs.
Capacity* (the Cup) reframes a local finding as systemic. *The Tent* is plain-language
tensegrity: a short rope pulls the whole structure, which is why "the problem is
never where the pain is." A finding stated first in this vocabulary stays one finding
as it crosses joint anatomy, the SBL, the Bladder meridian, and Sen Kalathari — and
arrives tagged with exactly how sure we can be about each. The skill on display is the
translation, not an anatomy recital.

## The evidence layer as a trust feature

The honesty layer is deliberately built *into the body* of every answer rather than
worn as a banner. Each cross-map claim carries its tier: established, location-
correlation, partially confirmed, tradition, or inquiry-only. The clearest proof of
the discipline is when it cuts against the framework's own interest — naming, in the
same Wilke 2016 review, that the Superficial Back Line is confirmed (3 of 3
transitions) while the Superficial Front Line is unsupported (0 of 4), with the
Spiral Line sitting in between (5 of 9). Langevin's acupoint–fascia overlap is
carried as a *location* finding shown in the dissected arm, never as validation of
qi. Sen lines are honored as tradition, their depth deferred to the source. A guide
that reports evidence this straight — even when it would be easier to overstate — is
one a practitioner can stand behind in a treatment room. That is the function of the
evidence floor: not to impress a judge, but to make every reading defensible.

## What this doesn't cover (the gaps)

- **One worked region, by design.** The public guide carries chronic low-back /
  posterior chain end-to-end at sampler depth. The other regions are built on the
  same shape in the private partner tool, not here — and the guide says so rather
  than fabricating a region it doesn't have.
- **No clinical use.** It's a research-and-translation tool for practitioners, not a
  clinician. It doesn't diagnose, prescribe, or replace a hands-on assessment.
- **The meridian and Sen layers are not validated science, and aren't claimed to
  be.** The location correlation is region-specific (the arm Langevin dissected); the
  posterior-chain extension is plausibility, not demonstration. The Sen lineage depth
  lives with the tradition and in Gabe's cards and books, not in this guide.
- **The interpretive layer (Cooley's emotional themes) is inquiry only.** It surfaces
  a question to sit with, never a cause. "Your back pain comes from an issue with
  support" is a sentence the guide will not write.
