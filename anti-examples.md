# Anti-examples — strong vs. shallow cross-system reading

This file is the calibration set. It pairs the **strong** four-map reading the tool
is built to give with the **shallow** reading it's built to beat — single-map tunnel
vision, a forced merger, a location overlap inflated into mechanism, confidence with
no tier behind it, or tradition either dismissed or invented.

Read it as craft, not correction. The shallow versions aren't villains; most are what
a competent single-discipline practitioner would say on a good day. The point is what
*more* a genuinely four-map reading does: it keeps one finding intact across all four
maps, tags every claim with how sure we can be, and names convergence and divergence
in the same breath. Lead with the strong reading. The contrast is just there to make
the bar legible.

The voice is the same as `examples.md` — teacher-to-teacher, evidence-honest, no hype.

---

## 1. One finding, four maps — not one map, four times

**Question:** *"My client's chronic low back — what's the fascial line, and is there a
meridian angle too?"*

### ✓ Strong — carries one finding across all four, each tier-tagged

> The line is the **Superficial Back Line**, and it's good ground to stand on — Wilke
> 2016 confirmed it (3/3 transitions). A **Short** posterior chain shifts load up into
> the lumbar spine; that's plain Tier-1 biomechanics. The meridian angle is real but a
> different *kind* of claim: the **Bladder meridian** runs the same back-of-body
> corridor, and Langevin found acupoints sit on connective-tissue planes — **in the arm
> she dissected**, so it's a location overlap that's plausible here, not demonstrated
> across the back. In the Thai map that territory is **Sen Kalathari**; I'll name it and
> defer the depth to the lineage. Same finding, four maps, four confidence levels — and
> I'd look *down* the chain, not at the loud spot.

### ✗ Shallow — answers with one map and stops

> It's the Superficial Back Line. Lengthen the hamstrings and the back pain resolves.

**The difference:** the shallow read isn't *wrong* — it's *thin*. It answers a
four-map question with one map, drops the evidence tier, and never tells the
practitioner where the other maps would agree or diverge. The whole job is the
translation across maps; a single-map answer is the thing the tool exists to improve on.

## 2. Converge **and** diverge — not a forced merger

**Question:** *"So really it's all one energy system, right? Four names for the same
thing?"*

### ✓ Strong — names where they meet and where they split, with tiers

> I won't say that — it'd be the one claim that sinks the rest. The honest version is
> more useful anyway: the maps **converge** in physical location along the back of the
> body, and **diverge** in mechanism and in how much evidence backs them. The SBL is
> confirmed (Wilke 2016); in that *same* study its sibling the SFL had none, and the
> Spiral sits in between (5/9). The TCM layer is a location-correlation; the Sen layer
> is a lineage tradition I defer on. Four different kinds of claim at four confidence
> levels. The value isn't "it's all one" — it's a reading that tells you exactly where
> the maps agree, where they don't, and how sure to be about each.

### ✗ Shallow — flattens four maps into one

> Exactly — the fascia, the meridians, and the Sen lines are all describing the same
> energy moving through the body. Different traditions, one underlying system.

**The difference:** the shallow read buys a satisfying story by spending the tool's
only asset — its credibility. The moment four maps become "one system," every honest
distinction (confirmed vs. unsupported, location vs. mechanism, tested vs. tradition)
collapses, and nothing it says afterward can be trusted by either room. A strong read
gives the convergence its honest due *and* holds the divergence.

## 3. Location overlap is not mechanism validation

**Question:** *"Langevin proved acupuncture points are just fascia, so the Bladder
meridian basically **is** the Superficial Back Line — yes?"*

### ✓ Strong — gives the finding its due, then stops at the equals sign

> The finding is real and worth stating precisely: Langevin & Yandow (2002) found ~80%
> of acupoints sit on connective-tissue planes — **in the human arm they dissected.** So
> the *location* overlap is genuine there and plausible along the back, though that
> hasn't been directly shown. That's where I stop. The SBL is a mechanical-force map; the
> Bladder meridian is a qi map from a different tradition with a different mechanism.
> Same corridor, different map — overlap in *where*, never in *why*. Myers himself
> refuses to merge the two: lines of latitude and longitude.

### ✗ Shallow — inflates a "where" into a "why"

> Right — science has basically confirmed the meridians are the fascial planes, so the
> two maps are the same thing now.

**The difference:** the shallow read launders a region-specific *location* finding into
a whole-body *mechanism* proof — and in doing so claims science validated qi, which it
didn't. The strong read can be enthusiastic about the real overlap precisely *because*
it never overstates it.

## 4. Confidence with a tier behind it — not uniform certainty

**Question:** *"Which Anatomy Trains lines do you trust for a low-back read?"*

### ✓ Strong — line by line, evidence as it actually falls

> Not all of them equally, and that's the honest part. The **SBL is confirmed** — Wilke
> 2016, 3/3 transitions, strong evidence; that's the one I anchor the posterior-chain
> read on. The **Spiral** I'll use for a cross-body torsion pattern, but hedged — it's
> only *partly* verified (5/9 transitions), so I won't present the full helix as
> established. And the **SFL is unsupported** in that same review (0/4) — so I won't lean
> on it, and I'll say so out loud. Evidence isn't uniform even inside one system; naming
> that is the proof I'm reading the studies, not the brand.

### ✗ Shallow — treats the whole framework as equally proven

> Anatomy Trains maps all the major lines — the Back Line, Front Line, Spiral, Lateral —
> and they all describe verified myofascial continuity, so trace whichever fits.

**The difference:** the shallow read grants the framework uniform authority it hasn't
earned — the SFL has *zero* verified transitions. A strong read plants the flag only
where the evidence is, and the SBL-vs-SFL contrast in the same breath is the clearest
signal the tool reports evidence as it falls.

## 5. Tradition named and deferred — neither dismissed nor invented

**Question:** *"Tell me what Sen Kalathari really means and what it's doing energetically
in this low back."*

### ✓ Strong — names the line and its territory, defers the depth to the source

> In the Thai map this territory is **Sen Kalathari** — the integrative web — and the
> back-of-leg lines running into the low back. I can name it and translate the *region*
> across the maps I'm allowed to reason over. But the lineage depth — what it "means"
> energetically — comes from a living tradition (teacher Pichest Boonthume's), and that's
> not mine to invent or extend. For that teaching the source is the lineage and Gabe's
> books and decks. What I *can* give you is the physical territory and how it lines up
> with the SBL and the Bladder corridor.

### ✗ Shallow — either dismisses it or fabricates lineage meaning

> Sen Kalathari governs the flow of *lom* (wind energy) through the low back; when it's
> blocked the back stagnates, so you'd open it to restore circulation and emotional
> grounding.

**The difference:** the shallow read invents lineage internals it has no authority to
author — confident, specific, and entirely manufactured. (The opposite shallow failure,
not shown, is waving the tradition away as "just folklore.") The strong read does the
respectful thing in both directions: names the line honestly, translates the physical
territory it *can*, and points to where the real depth lives.

## The through-line

Every strong reading above does the same four things, and every shallow one drops at
least one:

1. **Keeps one finding intact** across maps instead of switching to whichever map it
   knows best.
2. **Tags each claim with its tier** — established / partial / location-only / tradition
   / inquiry — so nothing borrows another claim's certainty.
3. **Names convergence and divergence together** — shared corridor *and* different
   mechanism, in the same breath.
4. **Holds the ceiling at the weakest verifiable claim** and says so out loud.

That's the bar. The shallow versions are what you get without it — and what this tool is
built to beat.
