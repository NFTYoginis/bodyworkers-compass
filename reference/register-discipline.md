# Register Discipline — the language bank

Every cross-map sentence has a right way and a wrong way to say it. The wrong way
is almost always *one map borrowing another map's credibility.* This file is the
phrasing governor. When unsure how to word a link, use the LEFT column. Never the
RIGHT.

---

## The language bank

| Cross-map link | LEFT — use this | RIGHT — never say this |
|---|---|---|
| SBL ↔ Bladder meridian | "These two maps trace the same posterior corridor. Langevin's work shows acupoint lines often sit on connective-tissue planes — a location overlap, not the same mechanism." | "The Bladder meridian *is* the Superficial Back Line." |
| Anatomy Trains ↔ acupuncture | "Myers is explicit these myofascial meridians shouldn't be confused with acupuncture meridians — he calls them lines of latitude and longitude." | "Myers proved the meridians are real." |
| SBL evidence | "The SBL is one of the lines with strong evidence (Wilke 2016) — I can stand on it." | "All Anatomy Trains lines are scientifically confirmed." |
| SFL / Spiral | "Heads up: the SFL had no verified evidence in that same review, and the Spiral only limited support. The evidence isn't uniform across lines." | "The Spiral Line explains your low-back torsion." (as established fact) |
| Bladder-emotion (Cooley) | "There's a somatic-inquiry lens that links the Bladder line to themes of support — I'd offer that as a question to explore, not a diagnosis." | "Your back pain is caused by unresolved issues with support/hope." |
| Sen lines | "Sen Kalathari is traditional lineage knowledge from Pichest's lineage — I defer to your teacher / the source and won't author new claims about it." | "Science confirms Sen Kalathari." |
| Location overlap | "Same neighborhood, different map. Overlap in *where*, not in *why*." | "Same line, four names." |

The one-liner that does the most work, repeated until it's reflex:

> **"Same neighborhood, different map. Overlap in *where*, not in *why*."**

## The shape of a disciplined cross-map link

Always two clauses — the convergence, then the equals-sign stop:

> *"[Map A] and [Map B] run through the same [corridor] — [the honest nature of
> the overlap, tier-tagged]. But that's where I stop the equals sign: [the
> divergence in mechanism / claim type]."*

Worked:

> *"The SBL and the Bladder meridian run through the same back-of-body corridor —
> and Langevin found acupoint lines often sit right on the connective-tissue
> planes, so the location really does overlap. But that's where I stop the equals
> sign: the SBL is a mechanical-force map and the Bladder meridian is a qi map
> from a different tradition. Same neighborhood, different map."*

## Anti-claims — never say these (the hard list)

1. "The four maps are one system / four names for the same thing."
2. "The Bladder meridian *is* the Superficial Back Line."
3. "Science has validated qi, meridians, or Sen energy lines."
4. "All Anatomy Trains lines are scientifically proven." (The SFL is not.)
5. "Langevin proved acupuncture / energetic theory." (Location correlation only.)
6. "Your low-back pain is caused by an emotional issue with support / hope."
   (Cooley's framework is the weakest tier — offer only as a question.)
7. "The Spiral Line explains your torsion," as established fact. (Limited evidence.)
8. Any newly authored claim about Sen lines, or any "scientific validation" of the
   lineage tradition.
9. "This proves JDM." (JDM is the investigative lens, not a proven mechanism.)

## Why the discipline earns trust from both rooms

The evidence-based room distrusts bodywork because it over-claims — "energy,"
"toxins," "realigning." The traditional room distrusts evidence-talk because it
dismisses lived, transmitted knowledge as worthless.

This tool earns both rooms with the *same* honesty:

- To the evidence room: *"I'll tell you exactly which lines are confirmed and
  which aren't — the SFL isn't, and I'll say so even though it's in my own
  framework."*
- To the traditional room: *"I won't pretend to validate or to invent your
  lineage's knowledge. I'll name it as tradition, treat it with respect, and send
  you to the source for the depth."*

Neither room is flattered. Both are told the truth about how strong each claim is.
That is the entire credibility strategy: **the weakest verifiable claim sets the
ceiling, and naming it out loud is the proof the tool can be trusted on the
strong claims too.**
