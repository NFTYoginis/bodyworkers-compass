## The finding (in the bridge vocabulary, before any map)

> Chronic low-back ache. Posterior chain reads **Short** from the heels up. Low
> back reports **Annoyed** around 4/10; hamstrings sit at **Background** — the
> client doesn't even feel them as the problem. Classic shape: *the pain is not
> where the problem is.*

Stated this way, the finding belongs to no single map yet. Now translate it
across four — tagging the tier at each step.

---

## Map 1 — Joint / skeletal-muscular anatomy · **Tier 1 (established)**

Lumbar spine, sacroiliac joint, and hip don't work in isolation. A **Short**
posterior chain — heels, calves, hamstrings — shifts load into the lumbar spine
and changes how the SI joint and hip share force. The ache shows up in the low
back because that's where the borrowed load *accumulates*, not where it
originates.

This is plain biomechanics. State it as fact. No energy claim needed, and none
made.

## Map 2 — Anatomy Trains · **the SBL, Tier 1–2 (confirmed for this line)**

That Short posterior line is the **Superficial Back Line (SBL)**: plantar fascia
→ calves / hamstrings → erector spinae → scalp. Mechanical force transmits along
it, which is exactly why short hamstrings can express as low-back strain.

**The flag, planted honestly:** the SBL is one of the lines Wilke et al. (2016)
found *strong* evidence for. I can stand on it. → *Sampler card 03-SBL.*

**The contrast, stated in the same breath:** in that same review, the
**Superficial Front Line had no verified transitions**, and the **Spiral Line**
only limited support. So if someone wants me to pin the low-back torsion on the
Spiral Line, I'll name it as a *possible* cross-pattern with weak evidence — not
as established fact. *Evidence isn't uniform even inside Anatomy Trains, and
saying so is the proof I report it straight.*

## Map 3 — TCM / meridian · **Bladder meridian, Tier 2 (location-correlation)**

The posterior-chain channel is the **Bladder meridian** — the longest channel,
running head-to-toe down the back body. → *Sampler card: Bladder, "The Performer
— the posterior chain in full."*

- **Location layer (Tier 2, real):** the Bladder channel runs through the same
  back-of-body corridor as the SBL. Langevin & Yandow (2002): ~80% of acupoint
  lines sit on connective-tissue planes. So the *location* genuinely overlaps —
  **but that's where I stop the equals sign.** The SBL is a mechanical-force map;
  the Bladder meridian is a qi map from a different tradition. Same neighborhood,
  different map.
- **Cooley's emotional layer (weakest tier — inquiry only):** Bob Cooley's
  framework reads the Bladder line as a theme of *honest support / hope*. I offer
  that only as a *question to sit with* — "is there somewhere you're feeling
  unsupported?" — **never** as "your back pain is caused by an issue with
  support." That sentence is off-limits (see anti-claims).

## Map 4 — Thai Sen lines · **tradition (outside the testable frame) — defer**

In the Thai map, this territory is:

- **Sen Kalathari** — the great integrative web; when the whole posterior pattern
  is involved, it lies across this line. → *Sampler cards Sen-01 / Sen-02.*
- **Nanthakrawat / Kitchanna** — the back-of-leg lines running from the soles up
  into the low back.

This is **transmitted lineage knowledge** (teacher Pichest Boonthume's tradition).
I name the lines and their physical territory — and I defer the depth to the
source. I do **not** author what the Sen "means," its clinical use, or its
internal teaching. For that: the lineage, and `gabeyoga.com/books` /
`gabeyoga.com/cards`. (See `rules.md`, Refusal Gate 4.)

## The investigative spine — JDM · **inquiry lens, not causation**

JDM doesn't add a fifth claim about the body; it's the *method* that made the
translation worth doing: **the problem is never where the pain is.** It's why we
looked at the heels and hamstrings when the low back was the loud one. Treated as
a way of asking — never as a proven mechanism, never as "this proves JDM."

## Re-integration (back into the bridge vocabulary)

Read as a **Tent:** the short posterior rope is pulling the whole structure out of
balance; the low back is just where the imbalance shows. Read as a **Cup:** the
low back is where the system's total load overflows. Lengthen the short rope
(posterior chain), and you change the pull on the low back — without ever having
claimed the four maps were one thing.

**The finding entered as one thing and stayed one thing.** Four maps described it
at four different confidence levels:

| Map | What it said | Tier |
|---|---|---|
| Joint anatomy | Short posterior chain loads the lumbar spine | **Tier 1** |
| Anatomy Trains (SBL) | The SBL transmits that force; confirmed line | **Tier 1–2** |
| Anatomy Trains (SFL/Spiral) | *Not* leaned on — unsupported / limited | **Tier 3** |
| Bladder meridian | Same corridor (location), different mechanism | **Tier 2** |
| Bladder (Cooley) | Support/hope theme — a question, not a cause | **inquiry only** |
| Sen Kalathari / back-leg lines | Named tradition; depth deferred to source | **tradition** |
| JDM | "Not where the pain is" — the method, not a cause | **inquiry lens** |

## Convergence point (the move on a single point) — **location convergence only**

A clean illustration of *connect-without-collapse* on one anatomical spot:
**Gallbladder-30 (Huan Tiao)**, the outer-hip depression, is named as a meeting
place in more than one map — a Gallbladder-circuit point, a junction near the
Lateral / Spiral lines, and an intersection on Sen Kalathari. One point, named in
several maps.

The disciplined reading: **this is a convergence in *location*, not proof the maps
are one system.** It's interesting that the traditions independently flag the same
spot — and that's *all* it is until evidence says more. The Spiral Line's
involvement here is limited-evidence (Tier 3); the meridian and Sen layers are
tradition. I present the convergence, and I hold the ceiling at the weakest claim.
