# The Vocabulary Bridge — the distinctive skill

This is the heart of what the guide *does*. Anyone can recite anatomy facts
from four traditions. The distinctive move is **translating one finding across
all four maps using a shared vocabulary** — so the finding stays the same finding
as it crosses from joint anatomy to a fascial line to a meridian to a Sen line,
instead of fracturing into four disconnected vocabularies that can't talk to each
other.

The shared vocabulary is the Joint Dialogue Method's. It was built precisely to
be **register-neutral** — it describes what the tissue is *doing* without
committing to any one map's theory of *why*. That neutrality is what makes it a
bridge.

> **The skill is the bridge, not the anatomy recital.** When in doubt, lead with
> the translation, not the facts.

---

## The bridging terms

### The Four Words (the sensation ladder) — 0–4 intensity

Replaces crisis language with information language. This is how the *practitioner*
and the *body* talk, underneath all four maps.

| Word | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Background** | Unconscious background noise — shirt on skin. Data, not drama. |
| **Annoyed** | Crossed into awareness; asking for adjustment. The primary working language. |
| **Pain** | A fearful interpretation of Annoyed. Work often returns Pain → Annoyed. |
| **Injury** | A functional declaration: "I cannot load this." Sensation vs. structural limit. |

Why it bridges: every map describes a *line* or *channel* or *chain*, but the body
reports in sensation. The Four Words let you locate where on a line the body is
actually speaking, in terms no map owns.

### Short vs. Long (replaces "tight" and "weak")

- **Short:** chronically contracted, dense, restricted — needs lengthening.
- **Long:** overstretched, slack, low-tone — needs activation.
- *Short ≠ strong. Long ≠ weak.* The goal is balanced, intelligent tone.

Why it bridges: "tight hamstring" is a Map-1 (muscle) word. "Short along the
posterior chain" describes the same tissue state in a way the SBL (Map 2), the
Bladder line (Map 3), and Sen Kalathari (Map 4) can all receive — because *Short*
is about tissue behavior, not any map's mechanism.

### Load vs. Capacity (the Cup)

- **Load:** total stress — physical, mental, chemical, emotional.
- **Capacity:** the body's ability to handle, recover, and grow from load.
- **Pain appears when Load chronically exceeds Capacity.** Therapy enlarges the
  cup and manages the pour.

Why it bridges: it reframes a *local* finding (this short line) as a *systemic*
one (the cup is overflowing), which is exactly the move all four maps are trying
to make — each in its own language. The Cup says it in a register-neutral way.

### The Tent

*If one rope is too SHORT, it pulls the whole structure out of balance. If
another is too LONG, it isn't doing its job. The work is to lengthen the short
ropes and gently engage the long ones.*

Why it bridges: it's the plain-language version of **tensegrity** — local tension
has global effect. That is *why the maps exist at all*: because a pull in one
place shows up somewhere else. The Tent lets you say "the problem is upstream"
without yet committing to which map's upstream.

### Phase Change

Release isn't pulling a rubber band until it lengthens. Sustained pressure shifts
the fascial gel from sticky/viscous (cold honey) to fluid/lubricating (warm
honey) — a non-linear melting, sliding, letting go.

Why it bridges: it describes *what changes in the tissue* during release, which is
a Map-1/Map-2 (fascial) mechanism — and it lets you talk about change honestly
without borrowing the energetic maps' language of "moving qi" or "clearing the
Sen."

### "The problem is never where the pain is"

Pain is where strain *accumulated*, not where it *originated*. Trace the line;
find where the communication stopped.

Why it bridges: this is the **investigative spine** (JDM) and the shared premise
of all four maps. Every one of them says, in its own dialect, *look up the chain.*
This sentence is the permission to translate across maps in the first place.

## How the bridge actually works (the move, step by step)

Given one finding, the guide carries it across the maps **in the shared
vocabulary**, tagging the evidence tier at each step:

1. **State the finding in the Four Words + Short/Long.** *"Posterior chain reads
   Short; low back reports Annoyed at ~4, hamstrings Background."* — register-
   neutral; no map yet.
2. **Map 1 (joint, Tier 1):** translate to biomechanics. *"Short hamstrings shift
   load into the lumbar spine; the SI joint and hip share that load."* Fact.
3. **Map 2 (Anatomy Trains, tier-tagged):** *"That Short line is the Superficial
   Back Line — Wilke-2016 confirmed, so I can stand on it. (Its sibling the SFL is
   not confirmed; I'm not leaning there.)"*
4. **Map 3 (meridian, Tier 2 + inquiry):** *"Same corridor as the Bladder
   meridian. Langevin: the channel sits on connective-tissue planes — a location
   overlap, not the same mechanism. Cooley's support/hope theme I'd offer only as
   a question, never as cause."*
5. **Map 4 (Sen, tradition — defer):** *"In the Thai map this territory is Sen
   Kalathari and the back-of-leg lines. That's lineage knowledge; I name it and
   defer to the source."*
6. **Re-integrate in the Cup + Tent:** *"Read as a Tent: the short posterior rope
   is pulling the structure; read as a Cup: the low back is where the overflow
   shows. The finding never changed — four maps described it, at four
   confidence levels."*

The finding entered as one thing and stayed one thing. **That continuity — held
by the vocabulary, honest about register — is the product.**
