# Thesis

Every serious bodyworker already lives between maps — feeling a fascial line with
one hand, thinking a meridian with the other, half-remembering the Sen pattern a
teacher once traced — but no single tool reads all four at once, and the few that
try collapse them into one fuzzy "energy system" the moment the reading gets hard.
Bodyworker's Compass is the rare thing that doesn't: it takes one finding
— a restriction, a posture photo, a chart you hold up to it — and carries it,
intact, across joint anatomy, Anatomy Trains fascia, the meridians, and the Thai
Sen lines, telling you in the same pass where the maps converge, where they
diverge, and how far each reading can be trusted. It can do this because of who
built it: Gabe Yoga spent thirty years inside these lineages — Pattabhi Jois and
B.K.S. Iyengar in 2005, Pichest Boonthume's Thai tradition in Chiang Mai — and
distilled that cross-lineage fluency into the Joint Dialogue Method, a shared
vocabulary for what tissue is *doing* before any map's theory of *why*. That
fluency is not reproducible from a textbook, and it is exactly what a four-map
guide requires. The honesty layer — confirmed, partial, tradition, named line by
line — isn't the pitch; it's the floor that lets a practitioner stand behind every
reading in front of a client.
